> Introduction: True excellence doesn't need to seek redress from this utilitarian world. These mediocre talents, happily huddled in their iron houses, are nothing but a group of comical shadows in the face of her serene demeanor.

On March 2, 2026, Professor Qian Jiayun passed away peacefully. Professor Qian was inherently humble and unassuming. Her departure was without public obituary or elaborate ceremonies. It wasn't until International Women's Day that I learned from a classmate at Yangming University that this outstanding woman, who came so close to the Nobel Prize, had humbly declined the world's offer on the eve of her seventy-sixth birthday.

I first heard of Professor Qian in a textbook. In my high school elective biology course, an article titled "History Cannot Forget the Contributions of Chinese Scientists to PCR" stood out, and the protagonist was none other than Qian Jiayun. In modern academic narratives, Kary Mullis, the inventor of PCR, rightfully received the 1993 Nobel Prize in Chemistry. It wasn't until later, when biotech companies were embroiled in patent disputes, that it was discovered the cornerstone of PCR—the heat-resistant Taq DNA polymerase—originally originated from a small paper published out of interest by a Chinese scientist, Chia-Yun Chien, during her master's studies in Cincinnati.

To those well-versed in today's shortcuts, such a discovery should have been a path to a Nobel Prize or a bargaining chip for a multi-million dollar patent. However, she simply included it in a short paper, casually published it, and then walked away. Years later, Mullis was awarded the Nobel Prize for PCR technology, and the biotech company earned $300 million from patent transfers. She, the "cornerstone," received no share of the profits from these powerful groups, working tirelessly out of love for science.

But what does it matter? In those yellowed court documents, she delivered a resounding slap in the face to those businessmen who sought to monopolize knowledge, embodying the power of "previous technology." Because of Qian Jiayun's existence, PCR became a global public asset; because of the commercialization of PCR, the Human Genome Project, originally planned for twenty or thirty years, was shortened to five: truth should never be aloof.

Back in 1975, when she received her doctoral scholarship and her future seemed incredibly bright, she resolutely abandoned her research in Cincinnati to pursue love, transferring to Iowa to study neuroscience with her boyfriend, Professor Zhang Nanji. Those idealistic scholars, perhaps obsessed with moralizing, would probably criticize her behind her back for "lack of ambition and intellectual decline." They are accustomed to putting a price on every inspiration and calculating the return on investment for every passion. If a discovery cannot be monetized, cannot become the core of a business plan, cannot be transformed into a stepping stone for upward mobility, then it is "useless," and the person who made it is destined to be a failure. But I must say, a successful person must at least be a person of flesh and blood.

Later, Professor Qian returned to Taiwan, dedicating herself to neuroscience at Yangming Medical College and founding the Umbilical Cord Blood Foundation. She used the hands that could have held a Nobel Prize to care for children suffering from leukemia. Her social media photos are almost entirely of her life with Mr. Zhang Nanji. Even after being labeled a "Nobel Prize snub," and even jokingly told by her husband that he "caused" her to lose the Nobel Prize, for her, the place that originally belonged to the Nobel Prize had long been filled with thirty years of harmonious marriage and mutual support. Ms. Qian smiled serenely, her eyes filled with happiness as she exclaimed, "God led me down another path, which is actually quite wonderful!"

Although Professor Qian was a scientist, she was a theist, a Christian. Until her death, she firmly believed that an all-knowing and all-powerful God had guided her throughout her life. She lived a vibrant life immersed in God's kingdom. We pride ourselves on being an atheist society, yet we are keen on creating countless idols. Once someone achieves extraordinary success, public opinion rushes to confine them to a box called "sublimity," shaping them into a symbol of "dedication and sacrifice." Their death then stirs up a storm of public opinion, despite their vehement resistance during their lifetime. This reminds me of Zhuangzi refusing the Chu king's envoy by the Pu River: In that temple, there was a "divine tortoise" that had been dead for three thousand years, wrapped in brocade and hidden in a bamboo box. It was undoubtedly incredibly noble, but did it truly wish to be worshipped in that high hall, rather than be a common tortoise "dragging its tail" in the mud, living a real life?

However, for Qian Jiayun, that academic achievement, enough to earn her "divine status," was merely a grain of sand in the riverbed of her life. She refused to be that worshipped dead tortoise; she wanted to be that free spirit, wagging its tail, living in the mud, amidst love, faith, and the ordinary warmth of everyday life.

In this bizarre narrative logic, once someone is elevated to a godlike status, they are stripped of their individual right to pursue happiness, and incidentally, their right to "make mistakes." We are accustomed to measuring their value with grandiose figures, claiming they are worth a thousand armies, as if their brains were a precision supercomputer that never crashes. However, when these "gods" are forced to endorse fanatical, irrational decisions—whether it's a dream in the fields or a brutal killing that defies common sense—tragedy inevitably follows.

The paradox is that gods cannot be wrong. When disaster becomes too heavy to conceal, as long as it's cloaked in the guise of "exploration," the blood and tears caused by ignorance and arrogance can be easily wiped away. The most fatal flaw of this logic is that we are always only allowed to learn from the bleached, shining points of the "god," while avoiding any discussion of the bloody lessons he left behind. Thus, the idol remains worshipped in its shrine, while the stone that tripped the "god" still lies across the road. Refusing to reflect on past mistakes and refusing to acknowledge the blind faith behind divinity turns history into a vicious cycle of repeated errors.

Even more cruelly, this grand narrative, in creating idols, first and foremost condemns their families.

Turning the pages of history, we see "heroes" sitting upright in courtrooms and working tirelessly in laboratories, but their dining tables and bedrooms are conspicuously absent. The world marvels at Judge Dee's brilliant judgments, yet no one cares about his wife's appearance; Hai Rui is portrayed in history as an unyielding blade, but his broken struggles within his family are brutally trimmed away, merely to make that blade appear even more menacing.

This blatant concealment of private life is, in essence, a masterful technique. It suggests that the price of greatness must be "homelessness." If a person reveals even a trace of affection for their wife and children, it is a desecration of "sublimity." This narrative renders heroes isolated and ashamed of ordinary people. By stripping heroes of their families, we have successfully created a sense of "emotional guilt" throughout society—as if private happiness is forever insignificant and shameful in the face of grand goals.

During the May Fourth Movement, there was a famous slogan: "Down with the Confucian shop, rescue Confucius!" We tore down the old Confucian shop, yet hastily built new laboratory temples, trapping living souls within. Now, don't we also want to tear down these cold statues, dismantle those ornate bamboo boxes, and return to that free-breathing "muddy field" all those living scientists and politicians trapped inside—those who love playing cards, love life, shed tears, and know when to retreat? What we want are not flawless white marble statues, but flesh-and-blood individuals, capable of love and affection by the river, able to feel warmth outside the laboratory.

In our education, there is always this grand narrative of teaching people to be "saints." To be like Yu the Great, passing his home three times without entering. We must be like Pavel Korchagin, abandoning our tender lovers. Everyone talks about materialism, but does the development of things truly depend on individual will? Will the Yellow River burst its banks instantly if we simply open the door to see our crying infants or hold our wives' hands? Must we, for the sake of so-called ideals, crush the vibrant love of our youth and let our hearts stop beating with an iron will?

In the examination hall, we recite the words of sages, memorizing standard answers word for word; in the laboratory, for "research results," we mold our personal interests into standardized molds. Such education cannot cultivate sages who "preserve heavenly principles and extinguish human desires." If pragmatism does not presuppose humanism, it only produces those who are "without heavenly principles and devoid of human ethics." The weak become a group of numb individuals who dare not express their true thoughts or pursue their personal passions; the hardened become a group of profit-driven individuals who take "success" as the sole measure and bind their personal value to external evaluations.

Ms. Qian Jiayun's life is precisely a gentle rebellion against this distorted narrative—she neither became a "saint" bound by symbols, nor did she degenerate into a mediocre person chasing fame and fortune. Instead, she cultivated her own flowers at her own pace in the fields of science and love. She proved with her life that, compared to the loneliness of standing at the pinnacle of the world, growing old together on this "other path" filled with love is a more moving kind of fulfillment. In that moment of choosing to pursue love and faith, she understood responsibility better than Yu the Great, and courage better than Paul.
